Last month, a friend asked me to look at an investment property in Kenilworth.

"The yield is 5.2%," he said. "That's solid, right?"

I pulled the data. Within 20 minutes, I found three problems he hadn't considered. The "solid" yield was actually closer to 3.1% once you accounted for reality.

This is what happens when we evaluate suburbs by headline numbers instead of data.

Today, I'll show you exactly how I evaluate a suburb before I even look at a single listing.

The Problem With Averages

When most people research a suburb, they Google "[suburb name] median house price" and call it a day.

Here's why that's dangerous:

Averages hide the distribution. A suburb with a $750K median could have:

  • Most properties clustered around $700-800K (tight market, predictable)
  • Half at $500K and half at $1M (split market, completely different dynamics)

Same median. Totally different investment thesis.

When I looked at Kenilworth, the median told me nothing useful. The distribution told me everything: there's a clear split between older units and renovated houses, and they behave like two separate markets.

The Three Numbers I Check First

Before I look at any listing, I pull three data points:

1. Days on Market (DOM) Trend

Not the current DOM. The trend over 12 months.

  • Decreasing DOM = demand outpacing supply (buyers competing)
  • Increasing DOM = supply building up (sellers competing)

Kenilworth's DOM has increased 18% over the past year. That's not a red flag yet, but it tells me I have negotiating room that didn't exist 12 months ago.

2. Stock on Market vs. Sales Rate

This is the supply/demand equation in its purest form.

Formula: Stock on Market ÷ Monthly Sales = Months of Inventory

  • Under 3 months = seller's market
  • 3-6 months = balanced
  • Over 6 months = buyer's market

Kenilworth currently sits at 4.2 months. Balanced, leaning slightly toward buyers. This matches the DOM signal.

3. Rental Vacancy Rate

This is where my friend's "5.2% yield" started to fall apart.

He calculated yield assuming 52 weeks of rent. But Kenilworth's vacancy rate is 3.8%—meaning ~2 weeks of vacancy per year on average. For older properties (which his target was), it's closer to 5.2%.

That's nearly 3 weeks of lost rent baked into the suburb's fundamentals.

The Real Yield Calculation

Here's how the numbers actually worked out:

Factor His Number Reality
Annual Rent $31,200 $29,580 (after vacancy)
Property Management (8%) Not included -$2,366
Maintenance (older property) Not included -$3,000
Insurance + Rates Not included -$5,500
Net Operating Income $31,200 $18,714
True Yield (on $600K) 5.2% 3.1%

The property wasn't bad. But at 3.1% net yield with a balanced market showing softening demand signals, the investment thesis changed completely.

What I Told My Friend

I didn't tell him not to buy. I told him:

  1. Negotiate harder. The DOM and inventory data says you have leverage.
  2. Budget for reality. Use 3.1% net yield in your cash flow projections, not 5.2%.
  3. Consider the newer stock. The vacancy rate for post-2010 builds in the same suburb is 2.1%—that's a full percentage point difference in yield.

He ended up negotiating $35K off the asking price and targeting a newer property. Same suburb, completely different outcome—because the data gave him a real picture instead of a hopeful one.

Your Takeaway

Before you look at a single listing in any suburb:

  1. Check the DOM trend (not just current—12-month direction)
  2. Calculate months of inventory (stock ÷ monthly sales)
  3. Find the vacancy rate (and segment by property type if possible)

These three numbers take 15 minutes to find and will save you from "solid yields" that aren't solid at all.
